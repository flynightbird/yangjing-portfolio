<section id="design-challenge">
  <header className="section-heading">
    <p className="section-index">02 / DESIGN CHALLENGE</p>
    <h2>A live meeting is a changing system, not a set of responsive screens</h2>
  </header>
  <div className="reading">
    <p className="lead">People, content, permissions, and available space change during a meeting. A fixed responsive layout cannot keep the right subject primary across sharing, whiteboarding, and changes in group size.</p>
    <p>The challenge was to preserve one recognizable interaction logic across four platforms while allowing each surface to make different space, density, navigation, and control tradeoffs.</p>
  </div>
</section>

<section id="system-strategy">

  </header>
  <div className="reading">
    <p className="lead">I used one decision model: identify the current meeting context, decide what information must remain primary, then resolve the interface state for that device.</p>
    <p>User goals and role logic remain stable. Density, navigation, panel behavior, and orientation adapt to the platform.</p>

<section id="whiteboard-workspace">

    <p className="section-index">05 / KEY EXPERIENCE</p>
    <h2>Whiteboard Workspace</h2>
  </header>
  <div className="reading">
    <h3>Context</h3><p>Participants create and review content while staying aware of an active meeting.</p>
    <h3>Problem</h3><p>The canvas, participant awareness, and meeting controls compete for limited space, especially in mobile portrait orientation.</p>
    <h3>Decision</h3><p>Make the canvas primary. In portrait, the upper region keeps a small participant view while the whiteboard occupies the lower workspace. The visible participant is resolved by a shared priority rule rather than by screen-specific exceptions.</p>
    <h3>Delivery</h3><p>The shipped layouts preserved creation space and meeting awareness across platform and orientation changes.</p>

<section id="information-layer">
  <header className="section-heading">
    <p className="section-index">06 / KEY EXPERIENCE</p>
    <h2>Real-time Information Layer</h2>
  </header>
  <div className="reading">
    <h3>Context</h3><p>Participants need live comprehension, translation, and transcript access during a meeting.</p>
    <h3>Problem</h3><p>Personal comprehension preferences and meeting-level transcription governance require different permissions and controls.</p>
    <h3>Decision</h3><p>Live Captions are an individual control. The Transcript Panel is started and stopped by the host, while participants can request activation. The in-meeting layer supports source-language selection, translation, bilingual output, and simultaneous interpretation.</p>
    <h3>Impact</h3><p>The complete in-meeting language experience shipped. Transcript data is exposed through the Customer API; Agora Meeting does not own customer-built post-meeting storage, copy, or download interfaces.</p>

<section id="reflection">

    <p className="section-index">08 / REFLECTION</p>
    <h2>Build cross-platform governance before the component library starts to drift</h2>
  </header>
  <div className="reading">
    <p className="lead">The delivery pace meant the component system was formalized late. Similar components were maintained repeatedly across client and Web surfaces, increasing the cost of keeping states aligned.</p>
    <p>In a second iteration, I would establish shared state definitions, ownership, and component governance earlier: one source for interaction rules, explicit platform exceptions, and a review process that tests behavior across all four surfaces before release.</p>
